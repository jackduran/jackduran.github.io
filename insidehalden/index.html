CARCERAL MASHUP

A Visual Investigation into the Hybrid Architecture of Carceral Humanism

BY JACK DURAN

I n the middle of a Norwegian forest, on the outskirts of a small village, lies one the largest, most-watched experiments in behavioral modification in the world. Halden Prison, otherwise dubbed by Time Magazine as the “Most Humane Prison in the World,” has through the strategic use of contemporary architectural design, sought to eradicate prison violence and “rehabilitate” incarcerated people within its walls through what it refers to as the “normality principle,” which holds that life inside the prison should reflect life outside of it as much as possible.

With a motto that says, “Punishment That Works,” Halden has designed and simulated a forested micro-society inside its massive concrete walls. While advocates of prison reform across the world look to Halden as a beacon for realizing the humane treatment of incarcerated people, it is important to interrogate the ways disciplinary enclosures of prisons are being increasingly redesigned as societies of control with Halden Prison as the leading 21st century model of new generation prison design in which control insidiously presents itself as freedom. The result of this simulated freedom is the disorientation of the people held inside, the virtual eradication of violence within these walls, and any challenge to the carceral state along with it.
